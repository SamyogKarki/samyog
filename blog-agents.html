Autonomous Agents in Unity with A* and Ant Colony Optimization

March 20, 2025 Samyog Karki Unity, C#, AI, Pathfinding

The project, in one paragraph

Three autonomous agents navigate a 3D city built in Unity, picking up items and delivering them across the map. Each agent has to figure out where to go, how to get there efficiently, and how to avoid stepping on the toes of the other two. I built the whole environment — streets, buildings, assets from Mixamo and Sketchfab — and implemented two classic AI algorithms to drive their decisions: A* for pathfinding and Ant Colony Optimization for route discovery.

Why A* for pathfinding?

A* is the workhorse for a reason. On a grid-like city with blocked tiles (buildings) and open ones (streets), it gives you optimal paths fast, and the heuristic is easy to tune for the kind of movement you want. I implemented it over a nav grid overlaid on the city, with each agent querying it on demand whenever a new delivery goal popped up.

The interesting part wasn't the algorithm itself — there's a mountain of good pseudocode online for that — it was integrating it cleanly with Unity's update loop. Recomputing a full path every frame is wasteful; recomputing never is dangerous. I ended up with a cadence: recompute on a new goal, recompute on blocked, otherwise just follow the cached path and check for obstacles locally.

Why Ant Colony Optimization as well?

ACO felt like overkill for a single path between two points — and it is. Where it earns its keep is when agents are discovering good routes across many deliveries, and their "pheromone trails" can bias future decisions toward paths that worked well. Over time the agents stop treating every delivery as a blank slate and start reinforcing useful routes, which looks surprisingly lifelike from the outside.

Tuning it was the hard bit. Too much evaporation and the agents forget everything between runs; too little and they get stuck on an early-discovered but sub-optimal path. I spent more time on the evaporation rate and the exploration-vs-exploitation balance than I did on actually implementing the algorithm.

Building the city

Assets from Mixamo handled the character animations; Sketchfab covered environment props. The city itself I laid out block by block in Unity — streets wide enough for agents to pass each other, buildings tall enough to make the space feel inhabited. Lighting and post-processing were the last 20% that make a Unity scene feel finished rather than like a student project.

What I took away

Two big lessons. First — classical AI algorithms are still incredibly useful for concrete problems. You don't need a neural network to make convincing, intelligent-seeming behaviour; good pathfinding and a bit of reinforcement go a long way. Second — the hardest part of game AI isn't the algorithm. It's the integration: the cadence, the edge cases, the moment when an agent gets stuck on a geometry seam and everything falls apart. Those are the bits that take the time, and they're what you only learn by shipping something.
