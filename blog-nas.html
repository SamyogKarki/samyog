Building a Personal NAS Server from Scratch

August 15, 2025 Samyog Karki Linux, Networking, Storage

Why build a NAS at home?

Cloud storage is convenient, but I wanted two things it doesn't give me: full control over where my data lives, and the hands-on experience of running my own always-on Linux box. A home NAS is a perfect small-stakes way to learn storage, networking, and system administration on real hardware — the kind of project where mistakes actually teach you something.

Hardware choices

I kept it deliberately modest — a low-power x86 machine, a pair of drives for redundancy, and gigabit networking. The temptation with NAS builds is to over-spec, but I wanted something that would run 24/7 without the electricity bill catching my attention. Silent, cool enough, and enough compute headroom to run a few extra services later.

The Linux side

The OS install is the easy part. The interesting parts are the decisions that shape how the thing behaves day-to-day: which file system, how to structure the storage pool, how to expose shares on the LAN, and how to keep it secure when it's only ever behind my home router. I ended up with a mirrored pool — slower than striped, but I'd rather lose performance than a drive of photos.

SMB handles file sharing for the Windows and mobile devices in the house. I also run a small always-on service or two on the same box — handy because the NAS is already there, already on, and already has storage attached.

Networking and access

On the LAN it's straightforward — static IP on the NAS, firewall rules that only allow the ports I use. What took more care was deciding whether to expose anything to the internet. The answer, mostly, is no — a WireGuard tunnel lets me reach the NAS from outside the house without punching holes in the firewall. That single decision removed an enormous amount of attack surface and let me stop worrying.

Backups, because RAID is not a backup

The mirrored pool protects me against a single disk failure. It doesn't protect me against accidental deletion, ransomware, or a house fire. So there's a second layer — offsite, encrypted, automatic. Tested, because a backup you've never restored from is not really a backup.

What I got out of it

Running your own hardware teaches you things a rented VM can't. You feel disk latency when you move files. You notice when a service pins a CPU. You learn to care about temperature and dust. It's a small project, but it's made me a better engineer — more practical, more respectful of the physical layer that all the clever abstractions sit on top of.
